\subsection{Computational Efficiency Analysis}
\indent
To address practical deployment considerations, we evaluated the computational efficiency of the TCN-Stochastic model in terms of training time, inference speed, and model size.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Computational Efficiency Comparison}
\label{table:computational_efficiency}
\begin{tabular}{|l|c|c|c|}
\hline
\textbf{Model} & \textbf{Parameters} & \textbf{Training} & \textbf{Inference} \\
& & \textbf{(per epoch)} & \textbf{(per batch)} \\ \hline
TCN-Stochastic (Base) & 49,797 & 2.1s & 0.7ms \\ \hline
TCN-Stochastic Enhanced & 77,002 & 5.8s & 1.2ms \\ \hline
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\indent
As shown in Table \ref{table:computational_efficiency}, the enhanced TCN-Stochastic model has 77,002 parameters (55\% more than the base model's 49,797), reflecting the additional AUAA, Dynamic Weight Adapter, Feature Importance Learner, and residual prediction components. Training time per epoch increases proportionally from 2.1s to 5.8s on CPU, with early stopping triggered at epoch 55, yielding a total training time of approximately 5 minutes. Inference latency (1.2ms per batch of 16 samples) remains suitable for real-time monitoring at minute-resolution. The model size and computational overhead are justified by the significant improvement in anomaly detection capability provided by the attention mechanism and dynamic weighting scheme.
