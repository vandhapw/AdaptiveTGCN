\subsection{Problem Statement}
Given multivariate time-series data $\mathcal{D} = \{(\mathbf{x}_t, y_t)\}_{t=1}^{T}$ collected from multiple real-world sensors, where each $\mathbf{x}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$ represents the measurements of $n$ features at time $t$, the raw input data can be formally expressed as:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{X}_{\text{raw}} = \{\mathbf{x}_t\}_{t=1}^{T}, \quad
\mathbf{x}_t =
\begin{bmatrix}
x_{f1}(t) \\
x_{f2}(t) \\
\vdots \\
x_{fn}(t)
\end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{n}
\end{equation}

The goal is to detect anomalous windows while providing uncertainty estimates and feature-level explanations. Formally, we seek a mapping $f_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}: \mathbb{R}^{W \times n} \to \mathbb{R}^{n} \times \mathbb{R}^{n}_{+}$ that produces both a predicted mean $\hat{\mathbf{y}}$ and a calibrated variance $\hat{\boldsymbol{\sigma}}^2$ for each feature, such that anomaly detection can leverage both reconstruction fidelity and predictive uncertainty.

\subsubsection{DBSCAN-Based Outlier Removal}
Real-world sensor data frequently contains sensor saturation artifacts (e.g., CO$_2$ readings at 9999 ppm) that distort normalization and model training. We apply DBSCAN~\cite{ester1996dbscan} as a preprocessing step to identify and remove such outliers. Let $\mathbf{X}_{\text{scaled}}$ denote the standardized input. DBSCAN clusters points in the $n$-dimensional feature space with neighborhood radius $\epsilon$ and minimum density $\text{min\_pts}$:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{N}_\epsilon(\mathbf{x}_i) = \{\mathbf{x}_j : \|\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j\| \leq \epsilon\}
\end{equation}

Points with $|\mathcal{N}_\epsilon(\mathbf{x}_i)| < \text{min\_pts}$ are labeled as noise ($\text{label} = -1$) and excluded:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{X}_{\text{clean}} = \{\mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbf{X}_{\text{scaled}} : \text{label}(\mathbf{x}_i) \neq -1\}
\end{equation}

We use $\epsilon = 0.5$ and $\text{min\_pts} = 5$, which effectively removes saturation artifacts while preserving genuine sensor variation. In our dataset, this filter removes approximately 0.1\% of samples containing the anomalous 9999 ppm CO$_2$ readings.

\subsubsection{Sliding Window Segmentation}
The preprocessed data is partitioned into overlapping windows of length $W$ with stride $s$:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{X}^{(i)} = \begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{x}_{t_i} \\
\mathbf{x}_{t_i + 1} \\
\vdots \\
\mathbf{x}_{t_i + W - 1}
\end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{W \times n}, \quad t_i = i \cdot s
\end{equation}

For the base model, $W = 20, s = 2$; for the enhanced model, $W = 60, s = 3$, providing longer temporal context for the attention mechanism.

\subsection{Enhanced TCN-Stochastic Architecture}

The proposed TCN-Stochastic Enhanced framework combines temporal convolutional networks with probabilistic modeling and four novel components: (1)~Residual Prediction with zero-initialized output, (2)~Adaptive Uncertainty-Aware Attention (AUAA), (3)~Dynamic Weight Adaptation for hybrid anomaly scoring, and (4)~Feature-Level Anomaly Attribution. Figure~\ref{fig:STCN_workflow} illustrates the end-to-end pipeline.

\subsubsection{TCN Backbone with Stochastic Depth}

The backbone consists of three hierarchical temporal blocks with 64 channels each and exponentially increasing dilation rates $d_l = 2^{\,l-1}$ for $l \in \{1, 2, 3\}$. Each block stacks two dilated causal convolutions with weight normalization~\cite{salimans2016weightnorm}, ReLU activation, and dropout. The dropout layers serve a dual purpose: regularization during training and uncertainty quantification via Monte Carlo sampling at inference, following the Bayesian approximation framework of Gal and Ghahramani~\cite{gal2016dropout_bayesian}.

\textbf{Stochastic Depth.} We apply stochastic depth~\cite{huang_deep_2016}: each temporal block $l$ is randomly dropped during training with probability:

\begin{equation}
p_l = p_{\text{base}} \cdot \frac{l}{L}
\end{equation}

where $L$ is the total number of blocks and $p_{\text{base}} = 0.1$. When block $l$ is dropped, its output reduces to the residual connection only, encouraging redundant representations and improving generalization.

\textbf{Residual Connection with Learnable Scaling.} Each temporal block employs a learnable residual scaling parameter $\beta_l$, initialized to 0.5, to control the contribution of the skip connection:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}_l = \text{ReLU}\big(\mathcal{F}_l(\mathbf{h}_{l-1}) + \beta_l \cdot \mathbf{h}_{l-1}\big)
\end{equation}

where $\mathcal{F}_l$ denotes the convolutional operations of block $l$, and $\mathbf{h}_{l-1}$ is the input via the skip connection (with a $1 \times 1$ convolution if channel dimensions change).

\subsubsection{Dilated Causal Convolution}

Dilated causal convolutions~\cite{mehta_nac-tcn_2023} enforce temporal ordering by restricting each output to depend only on current and past inputs through left-only padding. Dilation rates $d_l = 2^{\,l-1}$ insert gaps between filter taps, enabling the receptive field to grow exponentially with depth:

\begin{equation}
\text{RF} = \sum_{l=1}^{L} (k - 1) \cdot d_l = (k-1) \cdot (2^{L} - 1)
\end{equation}

For $k = 2$ and $L = 3$ blocks with dilations $\{1, 2, 4\}$, the receptive field covers $1 + 2 + 4 = 7$ timesteps, sufficient for short-to-medium-range temporal dependencies within each window.

\subsubsection{Residual Prediction}

We employ a residual prediction strategy where the network predicts the \emph{delta} from the last observed timestep rather than the absolute value. Given input window $\mathbf{X}^{(i)} \in \mathbb{R}^{W \times n}$, we define the residual anchor as the final observation:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{x}_{T} \in \mathbb{R}^{n}, \quad T = W - 1
\end{equation}

The output fully connected layer is zero-initialized ($\mathbf{W}_{\text{fc}} = \mathbf{0}$, $\mathbf{b}_{\text{fc}} = \mathbf{0}$), so at initialization the model predicts the identity map $\hat{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{a} + \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{a}$. The final prediction becomes:

\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{a} + f_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(\mathbf{X}^{(i)})
\end{equation}

where $f_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ represents the TCN's learned delta. This formulation provides two key benefits: (1)~it creates a strong identity-like initialization that dramatically accelerates convergence, and (2)~it allows the model to focus capacity on learning \emph{temporal changes} rather than absolute magnitudes. Empirically, removing residual prediction degrades performance from $R^2 = 0.9965$ to $R^2 = 0.957$ (MSE increases from 0.000261 to 0.000382), confirming its architectural importance.

\subsubsection{Adaptive Uncertainty-Aware Attention (AUAA)}

Standard multi-head attention computes weights from feature similarity alone, ignoring that predictions with high uncertainty may be unreliable. AUAA addresses this by modulating attention with a learned uncertainty gate, enabling the model to down-weight uncertain timesteps and focus on reliable temporal patterns.

\textbf{Multi-Head Projection.} Given input $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times T \times C}$ with $H$ attention heads and head dimension $d_k = C / H$:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{Q}^h = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{W}_Q^h, \quad \mathbf{K}^h = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{W}_K^h, \quad \mathbf{V}^h = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{W}_V^h
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{W}_Q^h, \mathbf{W}_K^h, \mathbf{W}_V^h \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times d_k}$ are learnable projection matrices.

\textbf{Sequence Uncertainty Head.} Before computing attention, we derive per-timestep uncertainty estimates from the TCN output sequence using a dedicated network:

\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\sigma}_{\text{seq}} = \text{Softplus}\big(\mathbf{W}_{\sigma_2} \cdot \text{ReLU}(\mathbf{W}_{\sigma_1} \cdot \mathbf{X}_{\text{seq}} + \mathbf{b}_{\sigma_1}) + \mathbf{b}_{\sigma_2}\big)
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{W}_{\sigma_1} \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times (C/4)}$ compresses the channel dimension by a factor of 4 for efficiency, and Softplus ensures non-negative outputs. This yields $\boldsymbol{\sigma}_{\text{seq}} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times T \times 1}$, a scalar uncertainty per timestep.

\textbf{Uncertainty Gating.} The sequence uncertainty is transformed into a modulation gate through a learnable mapping:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{g} = \sigma\!\left(\mathbf{W}_g \cdot \text{ReLU}\big(\mathbf{W}_{g_1} \cdot \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{\text{seq}} + \mathbf{b}_{g_1}\big) + \mathbf{b}_g\right) \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times T \times H}
\end{equation}

where $\sigma(\cdot)$ denotes the sigmoid function (not to be confused with uncertainty $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$), and the gate outputs one scalar per attention head per timestep. The uncertainty modulation attenuates attention on high-uncertainty timesteps:

\begin{equation}
\tilde{\mathbf{A}}^h[t,:] = \frac{\mathbf{A}^h[t,:] \odot \mathbf{g}[:,t]}{\sum_{j} \mathbf{A}^h[t,j] \cdot \mathbf{g}[:,t,j]}
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{A}^h[t,:] = \text{softmax}\!\left(\mathbf{Q}^h (\mathbf{K}^h)^{\!\top} / \sqrt{d_k}\right)[t,:]$ are the standard scaled dot-product attention weights, $\odot$ denotes element-wise multiplication, and renormalization preserves the probability distribution. This ensures that attention weights on unreliable timesteps are suppressed while maintaining a valid probability distribution.

\textbf{Attention Residual Scaling.} To prevent abrupt perturbations from the attention mechanism disrupting learned TCN representations, we introduce a learnable residual scale $\alpha_{\text{attn}} \in \mathbb{R}$, initialized to zero. The attended output $\tilde{\mathbf{X}} = \text{AUAA}(\mathbf{X}, \boldsymbol{\sigma}_{\text{seq}})$ is blended with the original representation via:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}_{\text{final}} = \mathbf{x}_{\text{last}} + \tanh(\alpha_{\text{attn}}) \cdot (\tilde{\mathbf{x}}[-1] - \mathbf{x}_{\text{last}})
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{x}_{\text{last}} = \mathbf{X}[:, -1, :]$ is the last-timestep output of the TCN backbone and $\tilde{\mathbf{x}}[-1]$ is the corresponding attended representation. The $\tanh$ bounds the scale to $[-1, 1]$, and zero-initialization ensures the model starts as a pure TCN, gradually learning to incorporate attention as training progresses.

\subsubsection{Monte Carlo Dropout Uncertainty Estimation}

Following Gal and Ghahramani~\cite{gal2016dropout_bayesian}, we interpret dropout as approximate Bayesian inference. At inference time, $N$ stochastic forward passes with different dropout masks yield a distribution of predictions:

\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{y}}^{(n)} = f_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(\mathbf{X}; \mathbf{m}^{(n)}), \quad n = 1, \dots, N
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{m}^{(n)}$ is the $n$-th random dropout mask. The predictive mean and epistemic uncertainty are:

\begin{equation}
\bar{\mathbf{y}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N} \hat{\mathbf{y}}^{(n)}, \quad \sigma_{\text{epi}}^2 = \frac{1}{N-1}\sum_{n=1}^{N} \big(\hat{\mathbf{y}}^{(n)} - \bar{\mathbf{y}}\big)^2
\end{equation}

We use $N = 50$ MC samples for evaluation, with an additional uncertainty head that predicts per-feature variance $\hat{\sigma}_{\text{alea}}^2$ to capture aleatoric uncertainty. The combined calibrated variance is:

\begin{equation}
\hat{\sigma}_{\text{cal}}^2 = \big(\sigma_{\text{epi}}^2 + \hat{\sigma}_{\text{alea}}^2\big) \cdot \lambda_{\text{scale}} + \lambda_{\text{bias}}
\end{equation}

where $\lambda_{\text{scale}}$ and $\lambda_{\text{bias}}$ are learnable calibration parameters, clamped to $[\lambda_{\text{min}}, \lambda_{\text{max}}]$ with $\lambda_{\text{min}} = 10^{-3}$ and $\lambda_{\text{max}} = 10^{3}$ for numerical stability. The $95\%$ confidence interval is:

\begin{equation}
\text{CI}_{95}^{\pm} = \bar{\mathbf{y}} \pm 1.96 \cdot \hat{\sigma}_{\text{cal}}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Dynamic Weight Adaptation for Hybrid Scoring}

Conventional stochastic methods combine reconstruction error and uncertainty with fixed weights. In practice, the balance should shift: reconstruction error is more informative during stable operation, while uncertainty matters more under sensor degradation or high noise. The Dynamic Weight Adapter (DWA) learns this balance adaptively.

\textbf{Encoding.} Given reconstruction error $e_t$ and uncertainty $u_t$ at timestep $t$, independent encoders project each signal into a shared representation space:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z}_e = \text{ReLU}\big(\mathbf{W}_{e_2} \cdot \text{ReLU}(\mathbf{W}_{e_1} \cdot \tilde{e}_t + \mathbf{b}_{e_1}) + \mathbf{b}_{e_2}\big)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{z}_u = \text{ReLU}\big(\mathbf{W}_{u_2} \cdot \text{ReLU}(\mathbf{W}_{u_1} \cdot \tilde{u}_t + \mathbf{b}_{u_1}) + \mathbf{b}_{u_2}\big)
\end{equation}

where $\tilde{e}_t = e_t / (\bar{e} + \epsilon)$ and $\tilde{u}_t = u_t / (\bar{u} + \epsilon)$ are mean-normalized inputs with $\epsilon = 10^{-12}$ for numerical stability.

\textbf{Temporal Context.} A GRU~\cite{cho2014gru} captures temporal dependencies across windows:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{c}_t = [\mathbf{z}_e;\, \mathbf{z}_u], \quad \mathbf{h}_t = \text{GRU}(\mathbf{c}_t, \mathbf{h}_{t-1})
\end{equation}

\textbf{Weight Prediction.} The dynamic weight $\alpha_t$ is predicted as:

\begin{equation}
\alpha_t = \sigma\!\left(\mathbf{W}_\alpha \cdot [\tilde{e}_t,\, \tilde{u}_t,\, \mathbf{h}_t] + b_\alpha\right)
\end{equation}

where $\sigma(\cdot)$ is the sigmoid function, ensuring $\alpha_t \in [0, 1]$. The hybrid anomaly score blends the two signals:

\begin{equation}
s_t = \alpha_t \cdot e_t + (1 - \alpha_t) \cdot u_t
\end{equation}

When $\alpha_t \to 1$, the score relies on reconstruction error; when $\alpha_t \to 0$, it relies on uncertainty. In our experiments, learned weights cluster near $\bar{\alpha} = 0.454$ with $\text{std}(\alpha) = 0.006$, indicating that the uncertainty signal is too weak to drive meaningful dynamic weighting when residual prediction already provides strong baselines.

\subsubsection{Feature-Level Anomaly Attribution}

To make anomaly detection actionable, we need to know \emph{which} sensors triggered the alarm --- not just \emph{that} an anomaly occurred.

\textbf{Inter-Sensor Correlation.} A learnable correlation matrix $\mathbf{C} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ captures known physical dependencies (e.g., temperature--humidity coupling). Initialized as $\mathbf{C}_0 = \mathbf{I}_n + \epsilon_C \cdot \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ with $\epsilon_C = 0.1$, it allows gradient-based learning of cross-sensor relationships:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{X}_{\text{corr}} = \mathbf{X} \cdot \mathbf{C}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Temporal Aggregation.} The correlated features are aggregated over the temporal dimension:

\begin{equation}
\bar{\mathbf{X}}_{\text{corr}} = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \mathbf{X}_{\text{corr}}[t,:] \in \mathbb{R}^{n}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Learnable Importance.} The module computes per-feature importance weights via a two-layer attention mechanism:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{h}_f = \tanh\!\big(\mathbf{W} \cdot [\bar{\mathbf{X}}_{\text{corr}};\, \mathbf{E}] + \mathbf{b}\big)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
w_f = \text{softmax}\!\left(\mathbf{V} \cdot \mathbf{h}_f + \mathbf{b}_{\text{sensor}}\right)
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{E} \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$ is the per-feature reconstruction error vector, $[\cdot\, ; \cdot]$ denotes concatenation, $\mathbf{W} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h \times 2n}$, $\mathbf{V} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_h}$ are learned weight matrices, and $\mathbf{b}_{\text{sensor}} \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$ is a learnable per-sensor bias. The softmax ensures $\sum_{f} w_f = 1$. Weighted errors provide feature-level attribution:

\begin{equation}
E_f^{\text{weighted}} = w_f \cdot E_f, \quad f = 1, \dots, n
\end{equation}

In our experiments, the learned attributions are: VOC (21.8\%), Humidity (21.7\%), CO$_2$ (21.7\%), Dust (20.0\%), and Temperature (15.0\%).

\subsubsection{Composite Training Loss}

The model is trained end-to-end with a composite loss function that balances reconstruction accuracy, uncertainty calibration, and weight regularization:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{MSE}} + \lambda_1 \mathcal{L}_{\text{unc}} + \lambda_2 \mathcal{L}_{\text{cal}}
\end{equation}

where $\lambda_1 = 0.1$ and $\lambda_2 = 0.05$. The three terms are defined as:

\textbf{Reconstruction Loss:}
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{MSE}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \|\mathbf{y}_i - \hat{\mathbf{y}}_i\|^2
\end{equation}

\textbf{Uncertainty Regularization:} This term prevents the variance predictions from collapsing to zero by penalizing small variance values:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{unc}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \hat{\sigma}_i^2
\end{equation}

\textbf{Calibration Loss:} This term encourages the predicted variance to match the observed squared error, providing well-calibrated uncertainty estimates:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{cal}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \big|\hat{\sigma}_i^2 - (\mathbf{y}_i - \hat{\mathbf{y}}_i)^2\big|
\end{equation}

The MSE loss drives accurate predictions, the uncertainty regularization prevents variance collapse (encouraging the model to maintain meaningful uncertainty estimates), and the calibration loss aligns predicted uncertainty with actual errors. Together, these three terms produce predictions that are both accurate and well-calibrated for anomaly detection.

\subsubsection{Numerical Stability}

To ensure robust training and inference, several numerical safeguards are applied throughout the architecture:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{NaN/Inf protection:} All intermediate tensors are passed through $\text{nan\_to\_num}(\cdot, \text{nan}=0, \text{posinf}=10^4, \text{neginf}=-10^4)$ to handle edge cases from division by zero or log of zero.
    \item \textbf{Variance clamping:} Predicted variance is clamped to $[\sigma_{\min}^2, \sigma_{\max}^2] = [10^{-6}, 10^{3}]$ to prevent gradient explosion from extremely large or small values.
    \item \textbf{Calibration parameter bounds:} The calibration scale $\lambda_{\text{scale}}$ is clamped to $[10^{-3}, 10^{3}]$ and the calibration bias $\lambda_{\text{bias}}$ to $[0, 10^{3}]$ during inference to avoid degenerate scaling.
    \item \textbf{Softplus activation:} Both the uncertainty head and sequence uncertainty head use Softplus ($\text{Softplus}(x) = \log(1 + e^x)$) rather than ReLU to ensure strictly positive variance outputs.
    \item \textbf{Uncertainty dropout:} A separate dropout layer with rate $p_{\text{unc}} = 0.5 \cdot p_{\text{drop}}$ is applied only to the uncertainty head pathway, providing regularization specific to variance estimation without disrupting mean prediction quality.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Theoretical Uncertainty Bounds}

We provide provable bounds on false positive rates for anomaly detection using concentration inequalities.

\begin{theorem}
(Chebyshev-Based Threshold) For any random variable $X$ with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$:
\begin{equation}
P(|X - \mu| > k\sigma) \leq \frac{1}{k^2}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\textbf{Application to Anomaly Detection.} Given predicted uncertainties $\{u_t\}_{t=1}^{T}$, we compute a threshold $\tau$ that guarantees a false positive rate (FPR) of at most $\epsilon$:

\begin{equation}
\tau_{\text{Cheb}} = \bar{u} + \sqrt{\frac{1}{\epsilon}} \cdot \sigma_u
\end{equation}

where $\bar{u}$ and $\sigma_u$ are the mean and standard deviation of predicted uncertainties. For $\epsilon = 0.05$ (5\% FPR), this yields $k = \sqrt{20} \approx 4.47$ standard deviations above the mean, providing a distribution-free guarantee on detection performance.

\textbf{Calibrated Confidence Intervals.} We compute calibrated confidence intervals for predictions:

\begin{equation}
\text{CI}_{1-\alpha} = \big[\hat{y}_t - z_{1-\alpha/2} \cdot \hat{\sigma}_{\text{cal},t},\;\; \hat{y}_t + z_{1-\alpha/2} \cdot \hat{\sigma}_{\text{cal},t}\big]
\end{equation}

where $z_{1-\alpha/2}$ is the standard normal quantile (e.g., $z_{0.975} = 1.96$ for 95\% confidence), and $\hat{\sigma}_{\text{cal},t}$ is the calibrated standard deviation from Eq.~(17).

\subsubsection{Anomaly Detection and Thresholding}

Reliable anomaly detection requires separating true anomalies from normal variation using well-calibrated thresholds. We implement four complementary strategies:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Percentile-based:} $\tau_{95} = Q_{0.95}(\{s_t\})$ --- simple, non-parametric threshold at the 95th percentile of anomaly scores.
    \item \textbf{Adaptive:} $\tau_{\text{adaptive}} = \tau_{\text{base}} \cdot (0.8 + 0.4 \cdot \bar{\alpha})$, where $\bar{\alpha}$ is the mean dynamic weight --- thresholds tighten or relax based on model confidence.
    \item \textbf{Theoretical (Chebyshev):} $\tau_{\text{Cheb}} = \bar{u} + \sqrt{1/\epsilon} \cdot \sigma_u$ --- provides an FPR guarantee of at most $\epsilon$.
    \item \textbf{Distribution-free:} $\tau_{\gamma} = Q_{1-\gamma}(\{s_t\})$, where $\gamma$ is the expected contamination ratio.
\end{enumerate}
