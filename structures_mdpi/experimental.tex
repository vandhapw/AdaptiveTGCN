
\subsection{Dataset Characteristics}
\indent 
The dataset used in this study was obtained from indoor air quality sensors installed in a laboratory environment. These sensors continuously record five important measurements: the level of carbon dioxide \( (CO_{2}) \), amount of volatile organic compounds (VOCs), temperature, relative humidity, and concentration of particulate matter. Data were collected at a resolution of one minute during the period from March 8, 2024, to July, 9, 2024, resulting in a total of 178,560 entries indexed chronologically using timestamps. Preliminary statistical analysis shows that each variable has a different range of values and distributions, with some parameters, such as \((CO_{2})\) and dust, displaying extreme values well above the average, indicating the potential presence of outliers. 

\indent
To ensure that our data are accurate and representative, we first processed them to deal with extreme outliers by using DBSCAN clustering \cite{ester1996dbscan}, enabling it to identify normal distribution patterns while flagging rare anomalies. Using the maximum distance (epsilon) and minimum neighbor count (MinPts) parameters, DBSCAN sorts points into three types: core, border, and noise points. This classification relies on two specific equations:
\begin{equation}
N_{\varepsilon}(p) = \left\{\, q \;\middle|\; \mathrm{dist}(p, q) \leq \varepsilon \,\right\}
\end{equation}
\noindent
where $\varepsilon$ is maximum radius \textit{neighborhood} and $\mathrm{dist}(p, q)$ usually use Euclidean distance.

\begin{equation}
\left| N_{\varepsilon}(p) \right| \geq \mathrm{MinPts}
\end{equation}
\noindent
where $\mathrm{MinPts}$ is the maximum number of $\varepsilon$-\textit{neighborhood}  that must be fulfilled to form a \textit{cluster}.

\indent Raw sensor data values showed high averages and standard deviations, especially for \( (CO_{2}) \) and VOC, owing to the presence of outliers of 9999 . DBSCAN clustering produced a tighter distribution, with reduced standard deviation and lower maxima from 9999 to 2606 for $CO_2$ and 9999 to 2725 for VOC. The average values also decreased, The median and lower and upper percentiles remained consistent, indicating that the core distribution of the data did not change, but extreme variability was reduced. The final dataset is publicly accessible via an online repository at the following link: \url{https://raw.githubusercontent.com/vandhapw/datasets/refs/heads/main/df_normal.csv.}

\subsection{Model and Experimental Configuration }
\indent
The model used is a variant of the TCN that integrates Monte Carlo Dropout to estimate prediction uncertainty. A distinctive feature of the TCN is the use of dilated convolutions with an exponentially increasing dilation rate in each layer, so that the model's receptive field expands without excessively increasing the depth of the network. In this implementation, dilation starts at 1 in the first layer and, then increases to 2 and 4 in the subsequent layers. Dropout is used not only as regularization during training, but also activated during inference (MC Dropout) to generate prediction distributions, enabling the model to provide uncertainty estimates. The convolutional layer starts with weights set using a normal distribution. This means that the average (mean) weight is zero, and most weights fall within a small range around that value. To prevent overfitting, we used dropout, weight normalization, and early stopping. The model was trained using small batches of data to improve its efficiency. 

\indent 
The model configuration and experimental settings were determined through a trial-and-error approach, the final setup employed three TCN layers with 64 filters per layer, a kernel size of 2, and dilations of 1, 2, and 4 to capture temporal dependencies. Regularization was ensured through a dropout rate of 0.2 combined with weight normalization. Training used the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-3}$, MSE loss, batch size of 16, and early stopping with patience of 10 across a maximum of 100 epochs.

\subsection{Evaluation Methodology and Performance Metrics}
Several common metrics are leveraged to evaluate how well prediction models perform. These include Mean Squared Error (MSE), Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) and Mean Absolute Error (MAE) are metrics that illustrate the disparity between predicted  and actual values. Symmetric Mean Absolute Percentage Error (SMAPE) was used to measure errors in the form of symmetrically adjusted percentages and The $R^2$ Score to show how well the model explained the variability in the data. The equations for these  metrics are as follows.
\begin{equation}
    \text{MSE} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\text{MSE}} = \sqrt{ \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2 }
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \text{MAE} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} |y_i - \hat{y}_i|
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
   \text{SMAPE} = \frac{100}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{2|y_i - \hat{y}_i|}{|y_i| + |\hat{y}_i|}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
   R^2 = 1 - \frac{ \sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2 }{ \sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \bar{y})^2 }
\end{equation}

\indent 
The TCN-Stochastic model was optimized to express the prediction uncertainty by utilizing Monte Carlo Dropout during the inference phase. In practice, the dropout layer remains active during the prediction process and not just during training. Each batch of samples from the validation data was run 50 times with random dropout configurations, producing a distribution of prediction outputs that represented the model's probability at each time series instance. From the distribution of the 50 samples, two important parameters were generated: the mean prediction as a representation of point predictions, and the standard deviation prediction as the standard deviation of probabilistic predictions. The predictive confidence interval (e.g., 95\%) is calculated from the mean and std prediction, namely the prediction ± 1.96 times the standard deviation, so that at each time and sensor variable, a range of realistic possible values is available and can be flagged as an anomaly.


\indent
Throughout the implementation process, the model demonstrated excellent predictive performance for multivariate sensor data. Training was conducted on CPU and converged efficiently with early stopping triggered at epoch 55 out of 100 maximum epochs. The best validation loss achieved was 0.000274, representing a 16.97\% improvement from the initial validation loss of 0.000330 at epoch 1. The final training loss reached 0.000252, indicating strong convergence without overfitting. The model was trained on 41,602 sequences for training and validated on 8,914 sequences, each with 60 timesteps and 40 features (representing 5 sensor variables with stochastic mean and variance outputs, enhanced by lag features, rolling statistics, and temporal encodings). Figure~\ref{fig:training_losses_enhanced} illustrates the training and validation loss curves, showing rapid convergence within the first 10 epochs and stable plateau thereafter.

\subsection{Feature Values with Uncertainty}
\indent 
Visual analysis of actual versus predicted values highlights the model’s forecasting ability and uncertainty quantification. As shown in Figure \ref{fig:feature-timestep} (a), the TCN‑Stochastic model closely follows actual sensor signals across stable and volatile segments with minimal deviation, outperforming baseline models. While TCN and LSTM perform adequately, they often lag during abrupt changes, and GRU or RNN exhibit biases such as under‑prediction at $CO_2$ peaks or overshooting in valleys. Recurrent models also tend to smooth rapid fluctuations, causing delayed responses. Overall, models with larger receptive fields and uncertainty regularization, such as TCN‑Stochastic, deliver more accurate and robust predictions for noisy, nonlinear signals like $CO_2$ and humidity, though performance remains dependent on data characteristics. This underscores the advantage of integrating uncertainty modeling with convolutional architectures for complex environmental time‑series prediction. Panel (b) presents a comparative analysis of predicted versus actual values for CO\textsubscript{2} and humidity over 60 timesteps. Predictions are shown with shaded areas indicating uncertainty. The TCN‑Stochastic model closely aligns with actual signals, with narrow uncertainty in stable segments that expands proportionally to volatility. In contrast, TCN and LSTM display wider uncertainty bands with frequent deviations from actual values, while GRU and RNN often fail to capture change amplitudes and produce overly broad or uninformative uncertainty ranges.

\subsection{Anomaly Detection Analysis}
\indent 
The TCN-Stochastic model detects anomalies by leveraging reconstruction errors and uncertainty through an adaptive hybrid scoring mechanism. As shown in Figure \ref{fig:anomaly_analysis}(a), an adaptive threshold of 0.000437 separates normal behavior from anomalies, with most errors falling below this value and only a small tail exceeding it. Anomaly scores, derived from a weighted combination of reconstruction error and predictive uncertainty (with dynamic weights learned by the Dynamic Weight Adapter), classify windows above the threshold as anomalies, highlighted in red in Figure \ref{fig:anomaly_analysis}(b). Statistical evaluations in panels (c) and (d) show that 8,439 windows were normal and 475 anomalous (5.33\%), confirming the model’s efficiency and low false-positive rate. Boxplots further reveal low, narrow scores for normal windows and higher, dispersed scores for anomalies, validating the anomaly score as a robust discriminator that reliably separates the two classes.
