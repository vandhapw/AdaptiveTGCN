\subsection{Interpretation of Results}
The experimental results demonstrate that adding the uncertainty-aware components improves reconstruction fidelity \emph{and} provides downstream anomaly-scoring outputs in a single architecture. The Base TCN achieves MSE $2.20\times 10^{-4}$ on the held-out test split, and the Full Enhanced model improves this to $1.65\times 10^{-4}$ ($-25.2\%$), with corresponding $R^2$ increases from $0.9984$ to $0.9988$. The Enhanced model therefore should be claimed as both a better forecaster and a richer anomaly detector, rather than as a trade-off between the two.

The ablation study reveals the source of this gain. MC dropout alone leaves point-prediction MSE essentially unchanged ($+0.005\%$), confirming its role as an uncertainty estimator rather than a forecasting improvement. The auxiliary objectives (dynamic-weight regulariser and feature-attribution loss) supply the dominant share of the MSE reduction ($-26.25\%$ relative to Base when activated without AUAA). Adding AUAA on top yields near-equivalent point-prediction MSE ($-25.19\%$): AUAA's distinctive contribution is the uncertainty-gated attention mechanism activated at anomaly-scoring time, which is not directly visible in the time-averaged MSE.

\subsection{Comparison with Prior Work}
Compared to the stochastic TCN framework of Ghimire et al. \cite{ghimire_electricity_2025}, our approach differs in two key aspects. First, we introduce adaptive attention that modulates feature extraction based on uncertainty, whereas Ghimire et al. use standard TCN with post-hoc uncertainty estimation. Second, our dynamic weight adaptation enables contextual anomaly scoring, addressing the limitation of fixed weights in prior approaches. The indoor-air-quality experimental results support these architectural additions as interpretability and anomaly-scoring mechanisms rather than as a raw MSE improvement over the base TCN.

Recent attention-based architectures provide additional context for our design choices. TSA-Net \cite{wu2026tsa_net} demonstrates that two-stage attention improves detection of both point and contextual anomalies, a finding that aligns with our AUAA design philosophy. Similarly, the work of Chen et al. \cite{chen2025shap_transform} on SHAP-enhanced transformers for IIoT anomaly detection highlights the growing importance of interpretable anomaly scoring in industrial settings — a direction our feature-level attribution module directly addresses.

The theoretical bounds we provide distinguish our work from purely empirical approaches. The Chebyshev-based threshold guarantee ($P(\text{false positive}) \leq 1/k^2$) enables practitioners to select thresholds with known confidence levels, which is essential for safety-critical applications such as environmental monitoring and industrial control.

\subsection{Practical Implications}
The proposed framework offers several practical benefits for sensor network operators:

\textbf{Interpretable Anomaly Attribution.} The feature importance learner enables operators to identify which specific sensors contribute to detected anomalies. In our experiments, mean attribution is distributed across humidity (20.4\%), VOC (20.1\%), CO\textsubscript{2} (20.0\%), temperature (20.0\%), and dust (19.5\%).

\textbf{Adaptive Detection Sensitivity.} The dynamic weight adapter adjusts the balance between reconstruction error and uncertainty. In our experiments, the mean dynamic weight is $\bar{\alpha} = 0.527$ with operating range $[0.003, 0.537]$, demonstrating responsive context-sensitive gating.

\textbf{Calibrated Confidence.} The Enhanced model exposes uncertainty estimates alongside reconstruction error, allowing anomaly decisions to incorporate predictive confidence rather than relying only on deterministic error.

\subsection{Limitations and Failure Cases}
Despite its strengths, the proposed framework has limitations that practitioners should consider:

\textbf{Uncertainty Calibration Gap.} On the indoor air quality dataset, the empirical coverage of the 95\% predictive interval generated by $M=50$ MC dropout samples is below the nominal target (approximately 0.23 vs.\ 0.95). This indicates that the raw MC dropout intervals are over-confident; post-hoc temperature scaling or alternative uncertainty estimators (e.g., deep ensembles) would tighten the calibration without sacrificing the architectural benefits we report. Practitioners deploying the framework in safety-critical settings should pair the Chebyshev distribution-free bound with empirical coverage validation on their own deployment data.

\textbf{Dependency on Training Data Quality.} The uncertainty estimation relies on the assumption that the training distribution represents normal operation. If the training data contains undetected anomalies, the model may learn to treat them as normal, reducing detection sensitivity. We recommend rigorous data cleaning using DBSCAN clustering as described in the Methodology section.

\textbf{Computational Overhead.} MC dropout inference requires $N=50$ forward passes per prediction. This factor-50 latency is parallelizable: on a GPU, batched inference absorbs all $N$ passes simultaneously with minimal overhead beyond memory utilization.

\textbf{Limited to One-Step Feature Prediction.} The current architecture predicts the next timestep for each feature from temporal windows. Capturing richer inter-feature dependencies during prediction could improve accuracy for highly correlated sensor groups, which we leave for future work.

\subsection{Future Directions}
Several promising directions emerge from this work. First, the enhanced objective should be tuned further if lower reconstruction MSE is desired alongside uncertainty calibration. Second, extending the framework to capture inter-feature dependencies through graph neural networks could improve prediction accuracy for correlated sensors. Dynamic graph attention networks have recently shown strong performance for industrial multivariate time series anomaly detection by modeling evolving sensor relationships \cite{liu2025graph_tcn}, suggesting a natural integration path with our TCN backbone. Third, online learning mechanisms could enable adaptation to concept drift in streaming sensor data. Fourth, the growing body of work on explainable anomaly detection \cite{chen2025shap_transform} indicates that post-hoc interpretability tools such as SHAP can complement the intrinsic feature-level attribution already provided by our framework, offering practitioners multiple lenses through which to diagnose anomalies.
